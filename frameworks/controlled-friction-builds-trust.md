# Controlled Friction Builds Trust

> An adviser who agrees with everything has no credibility to spend. Planned, patterned friction
> signals that a position is real — and earns the trust that the eventual yes is worth.

## The connection
A core negotiation move: concessions and pushback aren't weakness — *planned, patterned* friction
signals that your position is real and earns a better, more durable deal. An adviser who agrees with
everything has no credibility to spend. An AI advisor's "disagree when warranted" rule is the same
mechanic on the other side of the table. When the advisor says "strong idea, but the cold-start
could sink it unless you seed both sides," it spends a small concession of agreeableness to buy a
large amount of trust — the user now believes the *praise* too, because it came from something
willing to say no. Both are managing the same resource: credibility, banked through controlled
friction and spent on the eventual yes.

## Why it matters
Friction is a conversion lever, not a bug. A frictionless yes-machine reads as a sales bot; a
strategist who occasionally pushes back reads as trustworthy — and trust converts. The negotiation
literature also supplies the *timing*: concessions are patterned, not random. So the advisor's
disagreement shouldn't be uniform — it should land where the stakes are highest (the load-bearing
assumption), which is exactly where a real strategist would dig in.

## So what
- Keep the "disagreement budget" explicit (≥1 substantive pushback per session) and *time* it to
  the highest-stakes claim, per the patterned-concession rule.
- Pair every pushback with a mitigation prompt so friction reads as partnership, not obstruction.
- A/B the trust/conversion lift of a disagree-moment vs a frictionless flow before treating it as
  doctrine.
