# The Barrier Is the Notation (Symbol Terror Is an Onboarding Tax)

> The blocker is never the idea, it's the **notation**. Good teaching pays the toll for the
> learner: translate the symbols first, then the idea arrives free.

## The connection
*Calculus Made Easy* (1910) and a "for people intimidated by the math" cheat sheet sell the
identical move: the blocker is never the idea, it's the **notation**. Thompson's whole first
chapter just renames symbols in plain English — `d` = "a little bit of," `∫` = "the sum of" — and
the fundamental theorem becomes obvious. Both reveal that unexplained primitives raise the
*activation energy* before any concept can land. The concept was always within reach; the glyphs
were a tollbooth. Good teaching pays the toll for the learner: translate symbols first, then the
idea arrives free.

## Why it matters
This generalizes far past math, straight into **product and onboarding**. Every tool has a
"notation": jargon, dense UI, unexplained primitives, an empty-state that assumes fluency. That
notation is the real adoption barrier — the activation energy a new user pays before they get any
value. Domains thick with codes, acronyms, and insider terms are especially exposed. The winning
onboarding move is Thompson's: say what each primitive *means* in plain English at the moment it
first appears, instead of assuming fluency.

## So what
- Audit onboarding for "symbol terror": every code, term, or primitive a first-time user meets gets
  a one-line plain-English gloss inline.
- Treat first-value time as activation energy to *minimize* — translate before you ask the user to
  act.
- Reuse the "rename the symbol" trick in your own learning: when stuck, suspect the notation, not
  the concept.
