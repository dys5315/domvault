# Chirality Is Positioning

> You don't always need new atoms (features). You need the mirror flip — the same molecule
> oriented so it reads as a different drug to the customer.

## The connection
Some pairs of molecules are enantiomers: identical atoms, identical bonds, mirror-image handedness
— yet one can be a mild topical remedy and the other a controlled stimulant. The *arrangement*,
not the composition, sets the effect. Positioning is the market analogue. Two offerings with
near-identical feature lists can mean opposite things to a buyer purely by how the frame is
flipped. Inverting a single axis — who pays, who the product is *for*, what the default
assumption is — can flip an arguably-identical service into something that signals the opposite.

## Why it matters
For go-to-market this reframes differentiation: you don't always need new atoms (features). You
need the mirror flip — the same molecule oriented so it reads as a different drug to the customer.
Cheap to build, expensive to copy, because a competitor can't flip without indicting their own
model.

## So what
- State your chirality explicitly: name the single inverted axis and let it carry positioning,
  rather than racking up feature parity.
- Audit any new offer with one question: is this a new molecule, or a handedness flip of an
  existing one? Flips are cheaper and often sharper.
- Beware the dark twin: a flip that reads as the *stimulant* to the wrong segment. Test the
  framing per audience.
