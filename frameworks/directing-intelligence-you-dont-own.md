# Directing Intelligence You Don't Own (Managing Up Is the AI-Steering Playbook)

> Getting good outcomes from an agent more capable than you that you **cannot command, only
> orient** — that's the same problem whether the agent is a strong manager or a frontier model.

## The connection
Managing up and steering a frontier model are the same problem. The classic manage-up behaviors map
almost one-to-one onto a prompt or harness: *Clarity* = a crisp task spec and definition of done;
*Context* = the situating background the model lacks; *Options-not-problems* = asking it to surface
alternatives, not a single answer; *Anticipation* = pre-loading the edge cases; *Calibration* =
matching detail and verbosity to the task; *Logic* = demanding the reasoning so you can verify it.
You don't dictate to a strong manager or a strong model — you frame, orient, and make the right move
easy.

The inverse completes it. "Leaders create dependency, not performance" is the **anti-pattern for AI
tooling**: a harness you can't work without and can't verify is failing the same way a
dependency-creating manager is. Good leadership builds capability and steps back; good tooling
should make *you* more capable and remain checkable — not a black box you're hostage to.

## Why it matters
This is a unifying skill: steering coding agents, designing automated workforces, and not becoming
the single point of failure as a system scales. The manage-up rubric is a ready-made checklist for
both prompting agents and delegating to people. The dependency warning is the design constraint on
every harness: verifiable + capability-building, never load-bearing-and-opaque.

## So what
- Turn the manage-up behaviors into a reusable prompt/delegation checklist (Clarity, Context,
  Options, Logic up front).
- Audit your AI harness against the dependency test: can you verify its output, and could you
  operate without it?
- Apply "build capability, step back" to teams and agents alike — design for self-sufficiency, not
  reliance on you.
