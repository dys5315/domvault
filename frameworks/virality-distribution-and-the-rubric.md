# Virality, Distribution & the Rubric

> Don't average your virality drivers — one fatal zero shouldn't hide behind four strong scores.
> And the content is only the 10%; distribution is the 90%.

## The pattern
A common model scores "will this spread?" by averaging a handful of drivers — emotion, identity,
timing, clarity, looping. Four failure modes recur in that kind of model:

1. **The equation shouldn't be an average.** A mean implies the drivers are *substitutable* — but
   emotional-but-confusing content fails. An average lets one fatal zero hide behind four strong
   scores. Fix: **weight** the load-bearing drivers higher, make it **multiplicative** so a
   near-zero on any driver tanks the whole score (a limiting-factor model), or treat **clarity as a
   gate** (pass/fail), not an additive term.
2. **The scores are post-hoc and circular.** Scoring cases *after* they're known to be viral is
   survivorship + hindsight bias. Validate by scoring *before* outcomes, and include false positives
   (high-scoring flops) and false negatives (low-scoring hits).
3. **The model ignores distribution — the bigger half.** Quality is necessary but not sufficient;
   seeding, network position, algorithm fit, and timing-luck decide reach. The post is the 10%,
   distribution is the 90%. Split the score into **Content Score × Distribution Score**.
4. **The scale needs anchors.** 1–5 is subjective; add a one-line behavioral anchor per score so
   it's a real rubric — graded, explainable — not a vibe.

## Why it matters
A virality scorecard is itself an instance of the rubric pattern: define weighted dimensions, score
against them, explain the score. Tightening it (multiplicative + anchored + a distribution factor)
turns it from a hindsight label into a predictive tool.

## So what
- The most viral asset usually isn't copy — it's a **shareable result artifact** (an output the
  user *wants* to post). Design the output to score high on the drivers itself.
- Ship the model as `min/weighted(drivers) × distribution`, with anchored 1–5 rubrics — then it's
  defensible and reusable.
