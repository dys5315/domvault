# Cold-Start Is a Change-Management Problem

> A two-sided marketplace cold-start is a population-level change-management problem — and the
> classic change playbook's eight steps map onto it almost line for line.

## The connection
Kotter's *Leading Change* is usually read as an internal-org playbook, but his eight steps are
really a recipe for moving a population from a comfortable status quo to a new behavior — which is
exactly what a two-sided marketplace cold-start faces. The mapping is tight: **create urgency** =
the sharp pain the product attacks; **guiding coalition** = the first anchor participants whose
presence makes the platform credible to the next cohort; **short-term wins** = the early, visible
payoff that proves the new behavior beats the old; **don't declare victory early** = a marketplace
looks alive after a few transactions and then stalls if you stop seeding; **anchor in culture** =
the network effect, where the new behavior becomes the default and self-sustains. Kotter's central
warning — *most change fails from low urgency or skipped steps* — is the same failure mode as a
marketplace that launches both sides at once with no wedge and watches both sit empty.

## Why it matters
The hard problem in a marketplace usually isn't the product — it's seeding two sides
simultaneously, the canonical chicken-and-egg. Kotter says the wedge *is* the "guiding coalition +
first short-term win." Concretely: pick the side with the sharpest urgency, manufacture an early
win for them that doesn't require the other side to be full yet (e.g., concierge/manual matching at
first), and only then open the flywheel.

## So what
- Choose one side to seed first by urgency, not symmetry; concentrate the launch there.
- Engineer a first short-term win that's deliverable with a thin other-side (manual matching)
  before automating.
- Treat "looks alive" as the danger zone, not the finish line — keep seeding past the first
  transactions.
