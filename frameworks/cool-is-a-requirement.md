# Cool Is a Requirement — iPod, Not Zune

> Treat desirability as a hard requirement, not a nice-to-have. We want to be the iPod, not the
> Zune.

## The idea
The Zune was, on a spec sheet, a fine MP3 player — and it lost to the iPod on taste, story, and
cultural pull. Functional parity loses to desirability. So "is this cool / would someone *want* to
be seen using it?" is a gating requirement alongside "does it work?" — especially for anything that
has to spread. Cool is what generates the attracting field that pulls users in for free.

## Why it matters
Even in B2B and infrastructure — where "cool" is easy to neglect — the *demo*, the *name*, the
*story*, and the *case study* are the magnetic surface. Buyers and operators still respond to a
product that feels modern and inevitable over one that feels like enterprise sludge. The viral/cool
layer is also the cheap distribution that the "90% is plumbing" lesson says is most of the win:
desirability is distribution you don't pay for.

## So what
- Make "cool/desirability" an explicit line in every product rubric and pre-read, not an
  afterthought — design the *reaction*, not just the feature.
- For unglamorous products, the **case study** and the **live demo** are the iPod moment — they
  must feel sharp, not like a bolt-on.
- Heuristic: if a feature or launch wouldn't make someone want to *show it to a peer*, it's a Zune
  feature — fix the magnetism before shipping.
