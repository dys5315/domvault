# Trojan-Horse GTM (The Free Wedge Sells the Case Study)

> A deliberately-free pilot isn't a discount — it's a distribution buy. Its real deliverable isn't
> the work performed; it's the measured, quotable proof that unlocks the channel that scales.

## The connection
A free or below-cost pilot looks like a services engagement, but its real deliverable isn't the work
performed — it's the **measured case study**. The pilot is a *distribution buy* disguised as a
giveaway: deliberately unprofitable, because the thing it purchases is proof, and the proof unlocks
the actual prize (a corporate channel, a marquee logo, an enterprise license whose economics dwarf
the wedge). This is land-and-expand where the land is intentionally free, because the expand is the
real business.

The Trojan-horse structure has a precondition that's easy to miss: the gift only converts if its
results are **instrumented to be quotable**. A free pilot that does the work but produces no clean,
attributable, before/after numbers buys nothing. So the wedge's success criterion is not "did it
work" but "can we *prove* it worked, in a sentence a buyer will repeat." That ties straight to the
rule that every gate must emit an event: the pilot's value is only realized if its outcomes are
captured as measured, citable events.

## Why it matters
A free or below-cost pilot isn't a discount — it's a marketing line item whose ROI is measured in
the channel it opens, not the revenue it books. The hard part of these businesses isn't the service;
it's manufacturing undeniable, transferable proof and routing it into the channel that scales.

## So what
- Score every "free pilot" by the prize it's buying (channel access, marquee logo, case study), not
  by its own P&L.
- Instrument the wedge from day one: define the 2–3 metrics the case study must show, and wire the
  events that capture them before the pilot starts.
- Only run the deliberately-free wedge when the expand path is real and large enough to justify the
  loss-leader.
