# Strengths Decay Like Moats

> A personal strength is just a moat at the scale of one person — an edge relative to an
> environment. When the environment moves, an edge can flip into a liability.

## The connection
Personality frameworks treat personal strengths as durable, near-permanent traits — find your top
few, build your life around them. Competitive strategy treats company moats the opposite way: a
live SWOT carries not just the edge but its **decay rate** — every advantage erodes as the world
shifts, and a moat that isn't widening is narrowing. A personal strength is just a moat at the
scale of one person. Both are an edge relative to an environment; when the environment moves, an
edge can flip into a liability (the meticulous perfectionist in a ship-fast org; the
consensus-builder in a crisis that needs a unilateral call).

So strengths aren't a fixed asset to be discovered once — they're a **position that must be
re-marked to market**. The right question isn't "what are my strengths?" but "what is my edge
*given how the world is now*, and how fast is it decaying?"

## Why it matters
Any tool that profiles a person's strengths inherits this trap: a static strengths report ages
into bad advice. Encoding a decay check keeps the output honest over time, and pairs with a
candid-advisor posture — the honest advisor tells you when a former strength is now holding you
back. It also generalizes moat-decay logic from strategy down to the personal layer: one substrate
for both.

## So what
- Any strengths assessment ships with a "does this still serve you?" decay prompt, not just a
  static profile.
- Tag each named strength with a context where it's an edge and a context where it's a liability —
  make the conditionality explicit.
- Re-run the check on a cadence (quarterly), the way a portfolio is re-marked, rather than treating
  the profile as permanent.
